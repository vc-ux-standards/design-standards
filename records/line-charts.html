Line charts
and trends

A line shows change over time, and because it encodes the value as position and slope — not length — a zoomed y-axis is allowed here, the honest counterpart to the zero baseline a bar must keep. The two liberties a line still owes the reader: label the range you zoomed to, and don't cherry-pick the window.

Our context

VC trends things over rolling periods all day — a quality measure climbing month by month toward a year-end target, enrolment, utilisation, readmissions. The movement that matters is often only a few points, and the current month is usually half-finished. The deciding pressure is the reader must see the real movement without being misled by an arbitrary crop or a partial period drawn as if it were complete.

The rule

Use a line for change over time. A zoomed y-axis is fine — label it — because slope, not length, carries the value. Default to a rolling 12 months; mark the partial current period so it isn't read as a fall; and break the line across missing data rather than interpolating through it. Past ~4 series, switch to small multiples; use an area only for a running total.

01 / Quick reference

Reading a trend honestly

A line's freedoms come with duties. The zoom that makes a small movement visible is legitimate; the crop that invents a movement is not. Match the situation to the move, and the line stays honest.

Situation Do Why
Values cluster in a narrow high band Zoom the y-axis to the data and label the range. Slope carries the value; a full 0–100 axis flattens the movement to nothing.
The current period is unfinished Dash or grey the last segment and note it. A solid line reads as a real drop, not a month with days still to come.
A gap in the series Break the line; don't interpolate across it. A drawn segment claims data you don't have.
Five or more series Split into small multiples. Overplotted lines cross and force the reader back into the legend.
A cumulative running total An area; otherwise a plain line. Fill implies accumulation — wrong for a rate or a level.
02 / The pattern in practice

The zoom, the partial period, and the tangle

Two failures account for most misread trends: a crushed axis that hides real movement, and too many lines in one frame. Both are obvious the moment the fix sits beside them.

Avoid
A1c control · axis 0–100%

On a 0–100% axis, a real four-point climb looks like a flat line at the ceiling. The movement that decides whether the measure clears its target is invisible. A line is not a bar — it doesn't owe the axis a zero, and pinning it there wastes the whole plot.

Use
A1c control · axis 82–90%

Zoomed to 82–90% and labelled, the same climb reads at a glance. The range is stated, so no one is fooled; the month-to-date point is dashed and hollow, so a half-finished period isn't mistaken for a drop. The freedom to zoom is honest only when the axis says where it starts — see reading the chart honestly.

Avoid
Six measures · one frame
Diabetes BP Depression Colorectal Breast Flu

Six lines in one frame is a tangle, not a trend. The lines cross, the colours stop separating, and the reader is left tracing a legend instead of reading a shape. No single measure's direction is legible.

Use

Split into small multiples, each measure keeps its own shape. A shared scale and one hue let the eye scan direction and volatility down the grid in a single pass — no legend, no crossing. Past ~4 series, this is the move.

03 / The reasoning

Why the line earns the zoom

A line's job is to show change, and change lives in the slope. Every rule below protects that: give the slope room to be seen, but never let the framing manufacture a movement that isn't there.

1

Slope is the value, so zoom is honest

A line encodes by position, not length. Cropping the y-axis moves the whole line but doesn't distort a single slope, so a labelled non-zero axis is fair — the opposite of a bar, whose length would start lying.

2

The window is editorial

What you crop to is a claim. A zoomed axis must show its range, and the time span must be honest — a start point chosen to flatter is the line-chart version of a lie. The reading honestly record sets the limit.

3

Don't fake continuity

A drawn segment asserts data. Break the line across a gap instead of interpolating, and dash the unfinished current period so it reads as in-progress, not as a fall.

4

Split, don't overplot

Past ~4 series a single frame becomes a tangle. Small multiples on a shared scale keep each shape legible; save one frame for the two or three series a reader genuinely needs to compare directly.

04 / What the field says

The trend conventions are settled

Line for time, and a truncated axis is allowed when it's labelled — the field agrees. The bar's zero baseline and the line's freedom to zoom are two sides of one principle: match the axis to what the mark encodes.

IBM · Carbon — closest to our world

Line for time; truncate with care

Carbon defaults time series to line charts and, unlike bars, permits a non-zero y-axis when it helps the reader see variation — provided the scale is clear. It is the exact shape of VC's measure trends.

line for time; labelled zoom
FT · Visual Vocabulary

Change over time is its own family

The FT treats "change over time" as a distinct purpose served by lines, columns and slopes — the family that governs a series read left to right. It is where the line-for-trend default comes from.

lines for change over time
Claus Wilke · Fundamentals

Axis ranges and honest gaps

Wilke is explicit that line charts need not start at zero, that the chosen range shapes the story and must be reasonable, and that missing data should break the line rather than be bridged.

non-zero axis; break on gaps
Stephen Few · trend legibility

Give the movement room

Few argues a trend line should be scaled so the meaningful variation is visible, not squashed against a baseline — the reason VC zooms a clustered measure instead of pinning it to 0–100.

scale to the variation
Alberto Cairo · How Charts Lie

The window can mislead

Cairo's caution is the other half: a truncated axis or a cherry-picked time range can manufacture a trend. The fix is disclosure — label the range and show enough history — not a mandatory zero.

label the range; honest window
Synthesis

The pattern for tools like ours

For VC's clustered, rolling measures: a line on a zoomed, labelled y-axis over a rolling 12 months, the partial period dashed, gaps broken, and small multiples past four series. Our usage should follow it.

the VC trend default
