Colour for data

Colour is the weakest channel the eye reads, so it earns its place only when it does one job: separate categories, show magnitude, or show deviation from a target. VC already ships all three palettes — this record says which to reach for, and the two rules that keep colour honest: never as decoration, never as the only encoding.

Our context

VC's dashboards mix all three at once — categorical series (lines of business, measure domains), magnitude ramps (a risk score low to high), and to-target views (a rate above or below its benchmark) — read at a glance by analysts and care managers, not chart specialists. The deciding pressure is colour has to survive a legend-free glance and a colour-blind reader — roughly 8% of men — or it isn't carrying the data, it's decorating it.

The rule

Spend colour on exactly one job per chart: separate categories (the categorical set, capped at ~6), show magnitude (the sequential ramp, one hue low→high), or show deviation from a target or midpoint (the diverging ramp). Never as decoration, never as the only encoding — pair it with position, a label, or a shape. And reserve the attention hue for "needs attention" consistently: one hue means one thing across the whole dashboard.

01 / Quick reference

Three palettes, three jobs

Match the palette to the shape of the data, not to taste. The three scales already live in the tokens — pick by what the colour has to say, and stop reaching for hue once position or a label can carry it. All three are built on a colour-blind-safe (Okabe–Ito) basis.

Palette Use it for Tokens
Categorical Distinct, unordered series — lines of business, measure domains. Cap at ~6; past that colour stops separating. --cat-1…6 · classes .viz-c1…6

Diverging Deviation either side of a meaningful midpoint — under ↔ over a target, change up ↔ down. --div-low / --div-mid / --div-high
02 / The pattern in practice

Too many hues, and the flipped meaning

Two colour failures account for most bad charts in review: asking hue to separate more categories than the eye can hold, and letting the same colour mean different things across a dashboard. Both read instantly once the fix sits beside them.

Avoid
Members by contract type · 8 colours
Commercial Medicare Medicaid Dual Exchange ACA TRICARE Other

Eight segments, and the last two repeat hues the first two already used. Past ~6 categories the palette runs out of separable colours; the reader is stuck matching faint bands to a legend, and two "Commercial" blues aren't even the same series.

Use
Members by contract type · direct-labelled

Position separates the categories, so one hue is enough. A direct label carries each name and value; colour does no work here and is spent to zero. No legend, no colour-matching, and it reads the same in greyscale.

Avoid
Two measures · coloured by raw value
Breast screening
82%
▲ higher = green
Readmission rate
18%
▲ higher = green

Both tiles go green because both numbers rose — but a rise means opposite things. Higher screening is good; higher readmissions is bad. Colouring by raw magnitude makes green mean "good" in one tile and "worse" in the next, and the glance lies.

Use
Two measures · coloured by direction
Breast screening
82%
▲ toward target
Readmission rate
18%
▲ away from target

Colour tracks the good/bad direction, not the number. The attention hue is reserved for "moving the wrong way" whichever measure it is — so red always means the same thing, and the reader learns it once.

Sequential for magnitude · diverging for deviation Ramp set
Risk band and to-target
Risk score by cohort — sequential
Measures vs target — diverging

One hue for an ordered quantity; two hues off a neutral midpoint for deviation. The sequential ramp reads low-to-high without a legend; the diverging ramp puts "on target" at the calm centre so under and over fall to opposite sides. Never use a rainbow for either — hue order isn't magnitude order.

03 / The reasoning

Four rules that keep colour honest

Colour is powerful and cheap to overspend, which is why it goes wrong so often. Each rule below protects the same thing: that the colour a reader sees maps to something real in the data, and keeps mapping to it everywhere.

1

Colour is the weakest channel

The eye decodes position and length far more precisely than hue. Choosing a chart ranks the channels from Cleveland & McGill; colour comes last. Encode the deciding value on position or length, and let colour do the small remaining job.

2

Never the sole encoding

Pair colour with position, a label, or a shape. Roughly 8% of men can't separate the reds and greens, and any chart can end up printed or in greyscale. If removing colour destroys the chart, colour was doing a job it shouldn't have.

3

One hue, one meaning

Reserve the attention hue for "needs attention" everywhere. "Is higher better?" flips by measure — screening up is good, readmissions up is bad — so colour by the good/bad direction, not the raw number, or the same red contradicts itself across the dashboard.

4

Match the palette to the data shape

Categorical for unordered series, sequential for an ordered quantity, diverging for deviation. Never a rainbow for magnitude — hue order carries no low-to-high meaning, so the reader can't tell high from low without decoding a key.

04 / What the field says

The colour conventions are settled

Cap the categories, match the ramp to the data, and never rely on hue alone — every major source agrees. The three-palette split and the colour-blind-safe basis in VC's tokens come straight from this body of work.

IBM · Carbon — closest to our world

Three palette families, colour-blind aware

Carbon ships categorical, sequential and diverging palettes for exactly the three jobs, caps categorical series before hue stops separating, and documents colour-blind-safe usage — the model VC's tokens follow for a data-heavy enterprise product.

categorical · sequential · diverging
Okabe & Ito

A colour-blind-safe qualitative set

The Okabe–Ito palette is the widely-adopted qualitative set designed to stay distinguishable for deutan and protan viewers. VC's --cat series is built on its lineage, which is why the categorical palette survives colour-blind eyes.

deutan/protan-safe categories
ColorBrewer · Cynthia Brewer

Schemes chosen by data type

ColorBrewer classifies every scheme as qualitative, sequential or diverging and flags which are colour-blind and print safe — the canonical reference for "which palette for which data", and the source of the three-way split.

palette by data type
Datawrapper Academy

Practical colour do's and don'ts

Datawrapper's guidance is explicit: limit categorical colours, use one hue for sequential data, put a neutral midpoint on diverging scales, and never encode meaning in colour alone. Practical confirmation of the rules here.

limit hues · never colour-only
Claus Wilke · Fundamentals

The rainbow is the classic mistake

Wilke devotes a chapter to colour use and misuse: rainbow scales for magnitude mislead because hue order isn't quantity order, and too many categorical colours become unreadable. Both are anti-patterns VC avoids by construction.

no rainbow for magnitude
Synthesis

The pattern for tools like ours
