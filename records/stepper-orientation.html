When to use a vertical
versus a horizontal stepper

We ship both orientations. This sets out which to reach for, and why. The short version: default to vertical. The reason is specific to our flows — long, domain-labelled configuration — not a general style preference.

Our context

Our flows are long, server-backed configuration on desktop — cohort builders, quality-measure and risk-model setup — with step content always in a working canvas. Both orientations sit in that canvas, so placement decides nothing. What decides it is scale, per-step status, and retuning out of order.

The rule

Default to a vertical stepper in a left rail, content in the canvas beside it: it scales past five steps and shows every step's name, description and status at once. Reserve a horizontal stepper for a short, strictly linear wizard — three to five terse steps, each its own page.

03 / The reasoning

Four forces, all pointing the same way for us

The decision is structural, not aesthetic. Four pressures act on our flows in particular, and for configuration work they all favour the vertical rail.

1

Scale against domain labels

Our step names are domain phrases — risk adjustment methodology, attribution window, numerator logic. A horizontal bar divides its width by the step count and truncates them by the third step; our flows routinely pass the five-step ceiling a bar can hold. Vertical gives each step the full measure and scrolls when it needs to.

2

Name, description and status, at once

Configuration steps are rarely self-explanatory. Each needs a one-line description and a visible state — complete, in progress, not started, or error. Only the vertical rail shows all of that for every step at once; a horizontal bar shows a number and a word, and hides which sections are still invalid.

3

A persistent map, not a thin strip

A left rail is a map of the whole process, beside the canvas all session. It's the enterprise convention — Carbon places the progress indicator in side panels and tearsheets for exactly this. A horizontal bar is a thinner top-of-page overview, right for a short wizard, not a configuration session.

4

Non-linear retuning

Configuration is iterative — define a cohort, preview the population, adjust the criteria, check again. An interactive vertical rail makes every step a clickable target with its status, so jumping back is one click. A horizontal bar makes back-and-forth cramped and offers no per-step status to navigate by.

04 / Edge cases and anti-patterns

Where to draw the line in review

Reach for horizontal only when

  • The flow is short (three to five steps), strictly linear, and each step is its own page.
  • Labels are one to two words, with no description or per-step status to convey.
  • It is a focused, self-contained task, usually in a modal: a quick create, publish or confirm.
  • A compact progress overview at the top of the canvas is all the wayfinding the task needs.

Default to vertical when (most of our surfaces)

  • The flow has six or more steps, or groups into named phases.
  • Step names are domain phrases that need a description and a visible status.
  • Admins will revisit and retune steps out of order across a session.
  • The stepper should persist as a navigation map in a left rail beside the canvas.

Reject in review

  • Horizontal scrolling, or a horizontal bar that wraps to two lines, to fit the steps. Switch to a vertical rail and, past six or seven steps, group into phases.
  • Hiding step status. In a configuration tool, which sections still have errors is operational information the admin needs at a glance; surface complete, error and not-started states in the rail.
  • A stepper used for non-sequential navigation. Peer sections with no required order are tabs, not a stepper.
  • Contorting a horizontal bar for narrow widths. With negligible mobile use, do not compromise the desktop pattern; our only constrained contexts are side panels and split inspectors, which the vertical rail already fits.
05 / What the major design systems say

The enterprise convention, not a house opinion

The major systems converge on the same logic: vertical scales with steps, labels and status and reads more easily; horizontal is for short wizards where one step depends on the last. Carbon — IBM's system for data-heavy products — is the closest match to ours and the clearest on the rail pattern. Links are in the references.

IBM · Carbon — closest to our world

Vertical for readability, placed in a side panel

Carbon's guidance is to arrange the progress indicator vertically when possible because it reads more easily, and it explicitly places the component on full pages, in modals, in side panels and in tearsheets. It defines complete, current, not-started and error states, and an interactive mode that lets users navigate to any step, the exact behaviours a configuration rail needs.

vertical, side panel, status, interactive
Google · Material / Angular Material

Horizontal eats width and risks a second scroll axis

Material reserves horizontal for flows where a later step depends on an earlier one. Angular's implementation note is the operative reason to avoid it at scale: horizontal steppers take significantly more horizontal space and can introduce a second scrolling dimension, which makes content harder to consume.

horizontal only for dependent steps
Atlassian Design System

Short labels, and cap the steps

Progress tracker labels stay to one or two words on a single line. Beyond six steps, Atlassian advises simplifying or breaking the task into multiple flows rather than stretching the component, which is the basis for grouping our longer flows into phases.

short labels, group past 6
Ant Design — enterprise React

Vertical is the variant that carries descriptions

Ant ships horizontal, vertical and navigation variants. The vertical direction is the one that holds a per-step description, which is the layout our domain steps require.

vertical for descriptions
MUI · Material UI

Horizontal for dependent wizards

Horizontal is well suited when the contents of one step depend on an earlier step, the short page-swapping wizard where the bar tracks position and the work happens in the region below. That is our horizontal case, and only that.

horizontal for short wizards
Synthesis

The pattern for tools like ours

For an enterprise, desktop, analyst-facing configuration tool with long flows and step-level status, the field points one way: a vertical rail by default, horizontal kept to short linear wizards. Our usage should follow it.

vertical default for VC
06 / Recommendation for VC

Make the vertical rail the default

The house rule

  • Vertical, in a left rail beside the canvas, is the default for configuration and setup: cohort and registry builders, quality-measure definition, risk-model and attribution setup, contract arrangement, data mapping. These flows are long, domain-labelled, status-bearing and iterative, which is what the rail is built for.
  • Horizontal is reserved for the short top-level wizard: three to five terse, strictly linear steps, each its own page, usually in a modal. A quick create, publish or confirm.
  • Group any flow beyond six or seven steps into named phases in the rail rather than stretching either component.
  • Always surface per-step status, including the error state, in the rail. In a configuration tool that is operational information, not decoration.
  • Treat mobile as out of scope for steppers. Do not compromise the desktop pattern for a viewport we do not serve; the vertical rail already handles our only narrow context, the side panel.
