Reading the
chart honestly

A chart can be accurate to the last decimal and still mislead. This record settles the handful of distortions that keep coming up in review — a cropped axis, a dual scale, a raw rate passed off as performance — so the objection is a link, not a debate. It is the record the others point to when the question is "is this chart fair?"

Our context

VC's charts move money and clinical attention, and they are shown to executives, quality committees and contract partners who read the picture, not the query behind it. The deciding pressure is a chart that misleads a decision-maker is worse than no chart — so honesty is a standard we can cite, not a matter of taste.

The rule

These six are settled. When a chart breaks one, the fix — not the argument — is below:

  • Truncate the axis only on position marks. A line or dot may zoom; a bar or area starts at zero — length is its value.
  • One scale per frame. No dual axes; ship two aligned panels instead.
  • Say "percentage points" when you mean a point difference, not "percent".
  • Label raw versus risk-adjusted, and rank on the adjusted rate.
  • Suppress a rate on a tiny denominator, or show its N.
  • Show enough history that the window isn't cherry-picked.
01 / Quick reference

The distortion, and the fix

Most misleading charts in review are one of six moves. Each is fixable without losing the point the author wanted to make — the honest version is usually clearer, not blander.

The distortion Why it misleads The fix
Cropped axis on a bar Bar length stops matching value; a 3-point spread reads as a cliff. Start bars at zero. Zoom a dot plot or line instead.
Dual y-axes Two scales imply a correlation the author chose by sliding them. Two aligned panels sharing one x-axis.
"5%" for a 5-point move Percent and percentage points are different quantities; the reader can't tell which. Say "percentage points" for a point difference.
Raw rate, unlabelled A sicker panel reads as worse care; the ranking punishes case mix. Label raw vs risk-adjusted; rank on the adjusted rate.
Rate on a tiny N One case swings the percentage; noise looks like signal. Suppress below a floor, or show N. See numbers & states.
Cherry-picked window A short window hides the trend it's cut from. Show enough history for the movement to be read in context.
02 / The pattern in practice

The same data, told straight

Each distortion has an honest twin that survives a second look. The fix isn't to flatten the message — it's to move the point onto a mark that can carry it without lying.

Avoid
Screening rate · axis from 92%

A cropped bar exaggerates a three-point spread into a rout. Bar length is only honest from zero — crop the axis and length stops matching value, which is the one job a bar has.

Use
Screening rate · dots, 92–98%

A dot plot zooms honestly, because position — not length — carries the value. The axis is labelled 92–98%, the spread is real, and no reader mistakes a two-point gap for a landslide. The honest zoom is a dot, never a bar.

Avoid
Volume vs rate · two scales

Two scales on one frame manufacture a correlation. Slide either axis and the bars and line "track" or "diverge" at will — the relationship is the author's, not the data's.

Use
Volume and rate · aligned panels

Two panels on a shared x-axis let the reader judge the relationship. Each measure keeps its own honest scale; any link between them is read, not implied by a rigged overlay.

Avoid
Readmissions · raw rate

Ranked on the raw rate, Site C looks like the worst performer. But Site C carries the sickest panel — the chart is ranking case mix, not care, and the "worst" provider may be doing the hardest job well.

Use
Readmissions · risk-adjusted

Risk-adjusted, the order flips: Site C drops to mid-pack and Site A is the real outlier. Same providers, same period — the honest ranking sends the intervention to a different door. Always label which rate a chart shows.

03 / The reasoning

Why honesty is a standard here

Every rule below protects the reader's inference, not the author's convenience. In an analytics product the chart is the argument — and an argument that only works because the axis was cropped isn't one we ship.

1

Match the liberty to the mark

Position can zoom; length cannot. A line or dot reads by position, so a labelled non-zero axis is honest; a bar reads by length, so it must start at zero.

2

One scale per frame

A dual axis is a correlation you drew by hand. If two measures matter, ship two aligned panels; let the reader find the relationship instead of asserting it.

3

Say what you mean

Points are not percent, and raw is not risk-adjusted. Name the quantity and show the N; the numbers & states record sets how each is displayed.

4

The window is an argument

Where a trend starts and stops is an editorial choice. Show enough history that the movement can't be manufactured by cutting the axis at a convenient month.

04 / What the field says

The honesty rules are well charted

The distortions here are the classic ones, and the field is unanimous on the fixes. The value of the record isn't novelty — it's having one place to point when a chart in review needs to change before it ships.

Alberto Cairo · How Charts Lie

A field guide to the distortions

Cairo catalogues exactly these moves — truncated and dual axes, misleading ratios, cherry-picked ranges — and shows how each bends an accurate number into a false impression. It is the reference for treating chart honesty as a discipline, not an instinct.

the distortions, named
Claus Wilke · Fundamentals

Bars start at zero; position may zoom

Wilke is explicit that a bar's baseline must sit at zero while a line or dot, reading by position, may take a non-zero axis — the mark decides the liberty. His treatment of amounts and axis ranges backs the truncation rule here.

zero baseline; honest zoom
Datawrapper Academy

When truncating an axis is fine

Datawrapper's guidance draws the same line this record does: never crop a bar's axis, but a line chart may start above zero as long as the range is labelled. Practical confirmation of "match the liberty to the mark".

crop lines, never bars
FT · Visual Vocabulary

Honest defaults by relationship

The FT's chart doctrine pairs each relationship with the mark that shows it without distortion, and its editorial standards call out truncated axes and dual scales as things to avoid in published graphics.

distortion-free defaults
Cleveland & McGill (1984)

Why the mark decides the liberty

Their measured ranking of visual channels — position most accurate, length next — is why a dot can carry a zoomed axis that a bar cannot. The honesty rules rest on the same perception research as the choice of mark.

position > length
Synthesis

The pattern for tools like ours

For VC's committee-facing charts: crop only position marks, one scale per frame, name points vs percent and raw vs risk-adjusted, show the N and enough history. When a chart breaks one, cite this record and fix it. Our usage should follow it.
