Dot plots and
the honest zoom

When values cluster in a narrow band near the top of the scale, a zero-based bar chart flattens them into one wall. The fix is not to crop the bar — it is to change the mark. A dot plot encodes value as position, so a zoomed, clearly-labelled axis is honest where a cropped bar would lie. This is VC's record for the honest zoom: dots, lollipops, dumbbells and ranges.

Our context

VC ranks measures and providers that often sit within a few points of each other, high on the scale — screening rates in the 80s and 90s, gap-closure creeping toward a target. On a 0–100 bar axis those points vanish. The deciding pressure is the money-relevant delta — the point or two that moves a Star rating or clears a gap target — has to be visible without faking bar length.

The rule

For values that cluster, or bars that crowd past comparison, switch to a dot plot on a labelled, zoomed axis — position lets you zoom honestly. Use a lollipop when you want a little length back, a dumbbell for two states (before/after, actual-vs-target), and a range bar for an interval. In to-target work, sort by the gap, not the raw value. Always label the range; never truncate to manufacture a story.

01 / Quick reference

Which range form

Start from what the reader is comparing, then take the lightest mark that shows it on a common scale. Every form here reads value as position, which is why each can carry a zoomed axis without lying.

You're showing Form Why
Clustered values a zero-based bar flattens A dot plot on a zoomed, labelled axis. Position spreads the points that length crushed together.
A ranking of many near-equal items A dot plot — or a lollipop for a little weight. Dots decrowd where 20 bars turn into a solid block.
Two states per item A dumbbell — two dots joined by a line. The connector is the change; its length is the gap.
An interval or uncertainty A range bar — low to high on the scale. The span is the point, not a single endpoint.
One value against one target A bullet bar. See tile scale. A fill plus a target tick reads in one glance.
02 / The pattern in practice

The honest zoom, and sorting by the gap

Two moves recover a reading a zero-based bar throws away: put clustered values on a dot plot so the axis can zoom, and sort a to-target view by the gap the reader is chasing.

Avoid
Screening rates · zero-based bars

Eight rates between 86% and 94%, and the bars are one indistinguishable block. A zero baseline is right for a bar, so a bar is simply the wrong mark here — the reading the reader needs is exactly the part the zero-based length hides.

Use
Screening rates · dot plot, axis 80–100%

Zoomed to 80–100% and read as dots, the ranking is instant. Position on a labelled non-zero axis is honest — the dot never claims a length it doesn't have. The axis range is stated, so nothing is hidden. See the general rule in reading the chart honestly.

Avoid
Rate vs target · sorted by rate

Sorted by rate, the two widest gaps sink to the bottom of the list. The reader's job is to find where the shortfall is largest, but the sort answers a different question — who scores highest — and buries the answer that matters.

Use
Rate vs target · sorted by gap
Rate Target

Sorted by gap, the widest shortfalls lead — and the connector is the work to do. The dumbbell shows both the rate and the distance to target at once, ranked by the number the reader is chasing. Sorting is an editorial choice; see reading the chart honestly.

03 / The reasoning

Why position beats a cropped bar

A dot marks one value at one position; a bar draws that value as a length from zero. That single difference decides which mark may zoom and which may not.

1

Length starts at zero; position needn't

A bar's length is its value, so it must start at zero (see bars). A dot only marks a position on a scale — move the scale's window and the dot stays honest. That is the whole licence to zoom.

2

Label the zoom, always

A non-zero axis is only honest when it says so. Print the range on the axis; never crop silently to inflate a difference. The general rule lives in reading honestly.

3

Dots decrowd

Many near-equal items read faster as dots than bars. Twenty bars become a solid block; twenty dots stay separate points on a line — the reason Cleveland preferred them at scale.

4

Sort by what's being chased

Rank the view by the reader's question. In to-target work that is the gap, not the rate — sort by the shortfall so the widest ones lead, and the dumbbell shows both at once.

04 / What the field says

The dot plot is the settled alternative

When values cluster or categories grow, the references reach for the same mark: a dot on a common scale, not a cropped bar. It is the standard honest answer to exactly VC's problem.

Research · Cleveland & McGill

Position is read most precisely

Their graphical-perception work ranks position on a common scale as the most accurately decoded channel — ahead of length. It is why a dot plot on a zoomed axis beats a bar for values a reader must tell apart, and why Cleveland built the dot plot in the first place.

position > length
Claus Wilke · Fundamentals

Dot plots for clustered, long lists

Wilke recommends dot plots when a category list grows long or the values sit in a narrow band — the honest alternative to a cropped bar axis. He is explicit that only marks encoding position, not length, may take a non-zero baseline.

dots, not a cropped bar
FT · Visual Vocabulary

Dumbbells and ranges as their own family

The FT groups the dot-strip, dumbbell and range/bar under magnitude and ranking — two-point change and interval each shown as position, not two stacked bars. It is the source for the dumbbell-for-two-states default here.

dumbbell · range · dot-strip
Stephen Few · bullet graph

The single-value cousin

Few's bullet graph shows one value against a target and qualitative bands in the space of a line — the tile-scale relative of the dumbbell. VC uses it where the answer is one measure against one target rather than a ranked set.

one value vs target
IBM · Carbon

Dot and range in the enterprise set

Carbon — the system closest to VC's world — carries dot-plot and range patterns alongside bar and line, for comparisons where a bar would crowd or mislead. Confirmation that the dot plot belongs in a data-heavy product, not just in print.

dot · range in-system
Synthesis

The pattern for tools like ours

For VC's clustered rates and gap-to-target rankings: a dot plot on a labelled zoomed axis, a dumbbell for two states sorted by the gap, and a range bar for an interval — reserving the bar for values that genuinely run from zero. Our usage should follow it.
