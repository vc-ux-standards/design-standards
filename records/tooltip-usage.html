What belongs in a tooltip
— and what never does

A tooltip is a label, not a container. Ours carries one short line of helpful, non-essential text — a control's name, a shortcut, a term's definition. The moment it wants a second idea, a bullet, or a button, it has stopped being a tooltip; this record says where that content goes instead.

Our context

VC's screens are dense analytical surfaces worked with a pointer on large monitors, so a tooltip is cheap to reach and tempting to overload. The constraint that decides this record: a tooltip is invisible until asked for — nothing an analyst or admin needs to finish a configuration can live only there.

The rule (provisional)

A tooltip carries one plain line of non-essential text.

  • 1–2 short sentences, ~110 characters, 240px cap. No bullets, headings or images.
  • No buttons, no links. An action inside makes it a toggletip or popover.
  • Behaviour is fixed: hover and keyboard focus both open it, it stays while hovered, Esc dismisses it.
  • Never on a disabled control — the reason it's disabled goes in helper text on the page.

The deciding question is: can the user finish the task if the tooltip never appears? If not, the content moves down the ladder — helper text on the page, a toggletip, or its own page.

03 / The reasoning

Five forces keep the bubble small and inert

Every rule above falls out of one fact: the bubble is ephemeral and unfocusable. Five pressures, one direction.

1

Invisible until asked for

Nothing required to finish a configuration can live only behind a hover. A surface the user must discover is a surface some users never see.

2

It never takes focus

Focus stays on the trigger; the bubble is only announced. Any button or link inside is unreachable by keyboard — which is why none is allowed.

3

An ephemeral surface can't carry working content

The bubble lives only as long as the hover. Reading, comparing or copying from it fails the moment the hand moves.

4

The behaviour floor is fixed

WCAG 1.4.13 makes three behaviours mandatory, not preferences:

  • dismissible — Esc closes it without moving the pointer
  • hoverable — the pointer can travel onto the bubble
  • persistent — it stays until dismissed, never on a timer
5

Disabled controls swallow events

A disabled control fires no hover or focus, so its tooltip never reliably opens. Say why it's disabled in helper text on the page.

04 / Precedent and recommendation

The systems agree — the bubble stays small and inert

The systems are nearly unanimous, and the one dissent proves the rule. Brief plain text, no interactive content, behaviour fixed by the accessibility floor. Links are in the references.

IBM · Carbon — closest to our world

Text in a tooltip, actions in a toggletip

Carbon splits the pattern in two. The tooltip carries brief supplementary text on hover or focus — an icon-button tip is one or two words — and never holds interactive content. Anything clickable moves to the toggletip, the click-triggered sibling that takes focus and may carry buttons or links. That is precisely our split, with our own cap on length.

tooltip = text · toggletip = actions
Adobe · Spectrum

A few words, 160px, then escalate

Spectrum caps the bubble at 160px and a few words or one short sentence, and escalates anything heavier to its contextual-help popover. Tighter than our 240px, same instinct: the moment the content grows, the component changes.

160px · a short sentence
Atlassian vs Microsoft Fluent 2 — the disabled-control split

The one real split, and why we side with Atlassian

Atlassian keeps tooltips short and never attaches one to a disabled element — it cannot open for keyboard or touch users, hiding the reason from exactly the people who need it. Fluent 2 goes the other way and allows tooltips on disabled controls to explain why. We side with Atlassian, as Spectrum does: a disabled control swallows events, so the explanation goes in helper text on the page, visible to everyone.

never on disabled — helper text instead
Google · Material 3

Buttons in the bubble make it a dialog

Material alone allows buttons inside its rich persistent tooltips — and then wires the trigger aria-haspopup="dialog", makes the surface focusable, and dismisses on Esc. That is a popover in all but name. The exception concedes the rule: the moment a bubble holds an action, it stops behaving like a tooltip.

actions ⇒ aria-haspopup="dialog"
W3C · ARIA APG + WCAG 1.4.13

The accessibility floor

The ARIA APG is flat: tooltip widgets do not receive focus — so no interactive content, ever. WCAG 1.4.13 fixes the behaviour of anything that appears on hover or focus: dismissible, hoverable, persistent. This is the floor every rule above stands on.

dismissible · hoverable · persistent
Synthesis

One line, inert, never essential

For a pointer-first, analyst-facing product the field points one way: the tooltip is one short line of non-essential plain text with fixed behaviour, and anything essential, longer or interactive moves to the page or a toggletip. Our usage should follow it.

one line · inert · never essential

The house rule (provisional)

  • One plain line, capped. 1–2 short sentences, ~110 characters, max-width 240px. No bullets, headings or images.
  • Never the sole carrier of essential information. If the task fails without the tooltip, the content moves to helper text on the page.
  • No buttons, no links. An action inside makes it a toggletip — click-triggered, focusable, dismissed deliberately.
  • Never on a disabled control. The reason it's disabled goes in helper text, visible to pointer, keyboard and touch alike.
  • The behaviour is fixed. Opens on hover and keyboard focus, only on focusable elements; stays while hovered; Esc dismisses it.
  • Never restate a visible label — except on icon-only buttons, where the tooltip is the label.
