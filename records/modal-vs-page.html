Modal, full page,
or side panel

Three containers, one decision: what does the task do to the work behind it? A modal blocks it and demands resolution; a full page replaces it with a workspace; a side panel leaves it in place. Read that right and the container picks itself — the standing error is loading a multi-step form into a modal because it felt lightweight.

Our context

The same as everywhere in VC: desktop analysts and admins doing long, server-backed configuration in a working canvas. That sends heavy work to full pages, leaves modals for short interruptions, and makes the side panel the tool for work beside the main view.

The rule

Match the container to the task.

  • Modal — a short task that must interrupt: a destructive confirm, a one- or two-field create. A reversible action wants an inline confirm with undo, not a scrim.
  • Full page — anything long or multi-section that deserves its own URL.
  • Side panel — a focused edit that supports the main view and must stay visible.

The threshold: a modal past about four fields, or a side panel grown into a workspace, has outgrown its container — promote it to a page.

03 / The reasoning

Five forces behind the rule

The choice is structural, not a matter of taste. Five pressures decide it, and for our configuration work they sort the three containers cleanly.

1

A modal removes the page behind it

Blocking everything is the point for a confirm or a destructive check. It's wrong the moment the task needs the context the modal just hid, or the user must refer back to the data underneath.

2

Length and scrolling break a modal

A modal is a fixed, centred box. Load a multi-section form into it and it fails three ways:

  • scrolls inside its own frame
  • hides its own buttons
  • traps validation errors out of view

Our flows are long and validation-heavy — past 4 fields, or the first scrollbar, it belongs on a full page or a side panel.

3

Linkability favours a full page

A builder gets shared, bookmarked, and reopened to compare. A modal has no URL and no history entry — close it and the state is gone. Work that needs its own address belongs on a full page.

4

A side panel keeps the main view as reference

Much of our work is inspect-and-edit against a list or chart. A side panel keeps that main view in place so the comparison stays in sight. A modal would cover what the user is checking against; a full page would throw away their place in the list.

5

A loose dismissal loses the work

An accidental dismissal isn't an annoyance — it's lost configuration. Sessions are long, server-backed, and feed Care Management, Quality and Financial downstream, so each container defends differently:

  • a modal holding any entry never closes on a stray scrim-click or Escape
  • a side panel warns before discarding an unsaved edit
  • a full page autosaves or keeps a draft

Reach for a modal only when

  • The task is a single decision, a destructive confirm, or a create with one or two fields.
  • The user must resolve it before continuing, and blocking the page is the point.
  • There are at most two actions, and nothing needs to scroll.

Default to a full page when (most of our heavy work)

  • The flow is multi-section or long enough to be a real session: cohort and measure builders, the AI-filter registry builder, risk-model and attribution setup, contract arrangement, data mapping.
  • The user navigates within it and returns to it, and it should be linkable, bookmarkable and revisitable.
  • The work deserves the full surface rather than a centred box.

Use a side panel when

  • The task supports the main view and that context must stay visible: inspect a record beside a list, edit a value beside the chart it changes.
  • The work is a focused edit of a handful of fields, short to medium, and safe to treat as transient. The moment it grows into a multi-section workspace in its own right, it is a full page, not a panel.

Reject in review

  • A multi-step or multi-section form inside a modal. Promote it to a full page; if it must stay over the current context, a side panel.
  • A modal that scrolls, or hides its own action buttons below the fold. That is the signal it has outgrown the container.
  • Stacked modals, or a modal opening another modal, especially for a destructive path. Resolve one decision at a time.
  • A blocking confirm modal on a reversible action. If the user can simply undo it, an inline confirm with undo is lighter and less interrupting than a scrim that stops the page.
  • A side panel hosting a primary multi-section workspace. If it is the main task, not a companion to one, it is a full page.
  • A full page for a one-line confirmation. That throws away the user's place in their work for no reason; that is what a modal is for.
05 / What the major design systems say

The enterprise convention, not a house opinion

The major systems converge on the same logic: modals interrupt and stay short, complexity escalates to a full page, and a panel beside the main view carries context-bound work. Carbon — IBM's system for data-heavy products — is the closest match to ours and the most explicit about the ladder. Links are in the references.

IBM · Carbon — closest to our world

A complexity ladder from modal to full page

Carbon tells teams to use modals sparingly because they interrupt the workflow, and lays out an explicit format ladder for create and edit flows: modal, then side panel, then tearsheet, then full page, escalating with complexity. The summary it uses is that a modal is for a decision point and a tearsheet is for a process. Heavy, multi-step configuration lands at the full-page end, which is exactly where our builders sit. We take the ladder but not all four rungs: VC folds the tearsheet into the full page, holding our vocabulary to three so the choice stays cheap to make.

modal → side panel → tearsheet → full page
Google · Material 3

Escalate to full screen for complex tasks

Material calls dialogs purposefully interruptive and says to use them sparingly. Its rule for length is direct: reach for a full-screen dialog for complex tasks that group a series of inputs together before they can be saved. That is our multi-section builder, and Material sends it out of the modal.

full screen for complex, batched tasks
Ant Design vs Atlassian — the drawer split

The one genuine disagreement, and why we side with Ant

Ant keeps a Drawer so users can act without leaving the current page and stay in the context of the main task, its answer for heavier subtasks and forms. Atlassian is going the other way, deprecating its Drawer in favour of Modal. The split is about whether a product has a stable need to work beside the main view. Ours does, in every inspect-beside-a-list surface, so we keep the side panel.

drawer preserves context
Shopify · Polaris

Don't put complex forms in a modal

Polaris is the bluntest: do not use modals to display complex forms or large amounts of information; modals are focused, carry no more than two actions, and are temporary rather than persistent. Anything that needs to live on in the UI belongs on a page, not in an overlay.

focused, ≤ 2 actions, temporary
Nielsen Norman Group

Overlays interrupt; pages are linkable

NN/g's research is that modal overlays interrupt the user and block the content behind them, costs that are only justified when relevance and importance are high. Their recommendation for heavier content is to present it as a page instead, which also makes it easier to bookmark and share links, the linkability point in plain evidence.

page over overlay for heavy content
Synthesis

The pattern for tools like ours

For an enterprise, desktop, analyst-facing configuration tool, the field points one way: the full page is the default for builders and setup, the side panel carries context-bound inspect-and-edit, and the modal is reserved for short blocking decisions. Our usage should follow it.

page default · panel for context · modal for decisions
06 / Recommendation for VC

Make the full page the default for real work

The house rule

  • The full page is the default for builders and setup: cohort and registry builders — the AI-filter registry builder lives here — quality-measure definition, risk-model and attribution setup, contract arrangement, data mapping. These are long, multi-section, linkable and revisitable, which is what a page is for.
  • The side panel carries work that supports the main view: inspecting a record beside a list, editing a detail beside the chart it changes. Use it when the underlying context must stay visible, treat its state as transient, and keep it to a focused edit of a handful of fields; once it becomes a multi-section workspace, it has turned into a full page.
  • The modal is reserved for a short blocking decision that the user cannot simply reverse: an irreversible or high-consequence confirmation, a destructive check, a one or two field create. At most two actions, no scrolling, no steps. A reversible action gets an inline confirm with undo, not a scrim.
  • Promote a modal the moment it grows. Past about four fields or the first scrollbar, move it to a side panel if it must stay over the current context, or a full page if it is a session in its own right.
  • No stacked modals. One blocking decision at a time; if a path needs a second decision, sequence it, do not layer it.
  • Never let a container lose the work. A modal holding entry does not close on a stray scrim-click or Escape, a side panel warns before discarding an unsaved edit, and a full page autosaves or keeps a draft.
  • Three containers, by deliberate choice. Carbon runs a four-rung ladder with a tearsheet between side panel and full page; we fold that rung into the full page. Three types, not four, is one fewer thing for a builder to weigh and one fewer to litigate in review — the tearsheet's work is already covered between the side panel and the page.
