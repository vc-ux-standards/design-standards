Choosing a chart
for the question

Pick the chart from the question you're asking, then encode the value that carries the decision on the strongest channel there is — position or length, not angle, area, or colour. This is the entry point for VC's data-visualization standards: it routes each chart to its default form and links the record that governs it.

Our context

VC's analytics surface — dashboards, cohort and registry outputs, quality-measure and risk views — is read at a glance by analysts and care managers, not by chart specialists. Every chart has one job: answer one question accurately. The mark you choose decides how accurately it is read, before a single colour or label is set.

The rule

Name the question first — comparison, trend, part-to-whole, distribution, correlation, or hierarchy — then take the default mark for it from the table below. Put the value that decides the answer on the strongest channel available: position and length beat angle, area, and colour. Reach for an alternative form only when the row says to.

01 / Quick reference

Route the question to a mark

Start from the question, not the chart menu. Find the row that matches what the reader is trying to learn, take the default, and switch only under the stated condition. Each mark family has — or will have — its own record; the linked ones are settled.

You're asking Default Reach for instead
Comparison across categories A sorted bar or column chart. A dot plot when bars crowd; group a second category only if it stays to two or three.
Ranking — which is biggest A horizontal bar, sorted by value. Show the top-N and roll the tail into a grouped "Other" past ~12 rows.
Change over time A line. Area only for a running total; small multiples past four series.
Part of a whole A stacked or 100% bar. A pie only for two to four slices of a single whole.
Distribution of one measure A histogram or box plot. —
Correlation of two measures A scatter plot. Bin, or shade a heatmap, when points overplot.
Hierarchy or nesting A sortable table; a treemap if size is the point. A tree view for navigation — not for comparing values.
One value against a target A big number and delta, or a bullet bar. See tile scale. Never a shrunk axis chart in a tile.

Same data, two channels

The mark decides how fast the reader answers. Six contract types, one measure — members by line of business. On the left the value is carried by slice angle and area; on the right by bar length on a shared baseline. Only one of them answers "which is largest, and by how much?" in a single pass.

The same six as sorted bars: the ranking is instant. Length on a shared baseline lets the eye compare in one pass, direct labels carry the exact figure, and one hue is enough — position already separates the categories. No legend, no squint.

03 / The reasoning

Why the rule points this way

The choice is perceptual, not decorative. How accurately a reader decodes a chart depends on the channel the value is encoded on — and that ordering is measured, not a matter of taste.

1

The question picks the mark

Every chart answers one question. Name it — comparison, trend, part-to-whole, distribution, correlation, hierarchy — and the default mark falls out of the table above. A chart built to answer two questions answers neither one cleanly.

2

Position and length read most accurately

Cleveland and McGill measured how precisely the eye decodes each channel: position on a common scale first, then length, then angle, area, and colour last. Put the value that decides the answer on the highest channel the chart allows.

3

Colour is the weakest channel

Spend hue carefully. Use it to separate categories or show a scale, never as decoration; past roughly six categories colour stops separating and the reader is back to the legend. The Colour for data record sets the limits.

4

One chart, one job

Resist the combo chart and the dual axis. If two questions matter, ship two charts or small multiples — the reading honestly record settles why. A reader compares two clean frames faster than they decode one overloaded one.

04 / What the field says

Grounded in perception, not preference

The research and the practitioner references converge: start from the relationship you're showing, and default to the mark that encodes it on the most accurately-read channel. This is where the "position over angle" rule comes from, and why the pie above is the wrong default.

Research · Cleveland & McGill (1984)

A measured ranking of visual channels

Their graphical-perception experiments ranked how accurately people judge values by channel: position along a common scale is read most precisely, then length, then angle and area, with colour and shading least precise. It is the empirical basis for preferring bars to pies and for encoding the key value as position.

position > length > angle/area > colour
FT & BBC · Visual Vocabulary

Choose by the relationship

The Financial Times' chart doctrine groups every chart by the relationship it shows — comparison, change over time, part-to-whole, correlation, distribution, ranking, spatial, flow — and lists the marks that fit each. It is the model for this record's routing table.

Claus Wilke · Fundamentals of Data Visualization

A directory of forms, with cautions

Wilke organises chart types by purpose and is explicit about the failure modes — pies that can't be compared, dual axes that mislead, rainbow palettes that don't encode. The defaults here match his recommendations.
