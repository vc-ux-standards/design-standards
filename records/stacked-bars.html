Stacked bars, and what the tooltip carries

A tooltip is a disclosure tier, not a dumping ground: the values a decision turns on stay on the canvas, the tooltip only enriches them. And a segment too thin to see is still reachable — the hit target is the whole column, never the paint.

Our context

VC's stacked bars carry payer mix, gap status and risk tier — four to six categories across a handful of columns, read by analysts and care managers on large desktop monitors. The smallest segment is routinely the one that decides the work — the 2% cohort still open on a quality gap — and it is exactly the band the pointer cannot catch.

The rule (provisional)

Sort a value into the tier that has earned it. Every fact a decision needs is always visible; the tooltip is the second tier, not the first copy.

  • Always visible — the axis, the total, and a direct label for any value the decision turns on.
  • On demand (tooltip) — exact figures, denominators, definitions. Enrichment, never the only copy of a needed fact.
  • On navigate — the full row set, history, provenance. A table or a detail page, not a tooltip.

The hover and focus target is the column, not the segment. Give every column a hit band of at least 24px, whatever the paint does.

01 / Quick reference

Which tier holds the value

Three tiers, one rule: a value lives where a reader can reach it for the job it does. The names and order below are the rulecard's — cite the tier, not a paraphrase of it.

Tier What it holds Where it lives
Always visible The axis, the total, and a direct label for any value the decision turns on. Baked into the chart itself — direct labels, axis ticks, the total in the caption.
On demand (tooltip) Exact figures, denominators, definitions. Enrichment, never the only copy of a needed fact. The hover/focus card, anchored to the column it describes (§03).
On navigate The full row set, history, provenance. A table or a detail page, one click away — never a tooltip.
02 / What the tooltip carries

Enough to enrich, not to explain

A legitimate tooltip has a ceiling: one header, one total, four to six segment rows. Past that it has stopped enriching the chart and started standing in for a page the record hasn't earned.

03 / Where the tooltip sits

Anchored to the column it describes

The tooltip anchors to the column, sits above it, and never covers the mark it describes. The reader points at any part of Q1 and gets Q1's complete breakdown — so the shape being read stays visible while its exact figures are read off.

  • Above the column, with room reserved for it. The card clears the stack without covering it; its bridge remains inside the target, so the pointer can reach the card without dismissing it.
  • Clamped inside the plot. The first and last columns push their cards flush to the plot edge instead of overflowing it.
  • Reachable by keyboard and touch. Every column is a button: focus or hover opens its card, tap pins it, and Escape dismisses it.

04 / When the segment is too thin

Target the column, not the paint

One column is one target; it returns the column's complete breakdown. A 2% band is real estate no pointer can reliably land on — and it is not a separate control. The fix is never to enlarge the paint; it is to let any point in Q1 reveal Q1's full card.

Avoid
Payer mix · segment as target

If the hit zone matches the paint, a 2% segment is an impossible target. That precision beats what a mouse — let alone a touch point — can reliably deliver, on the exact band most likely to be the one that matters.

Use
Payer mix · column as target

The whole column is one target: its width is at least 24px and its height is the stack. Anywhere in Q1 opens the Q1 card; the 2% Dual paint remains evidence in that answer, not a target the reader has to land on.

Widening the band is the first fix, not the last. Work the ladder in order — each row is the next move, not an alternative to try first.

The segment What it allows When it still won't fit
A composition question; one column answers it Use one whole-column target: width ≥24px, height = stack, one complete column card. A named segment must be identified or compared across columns (next row).
More than 6 segments Cap the stack; roll the tail into a labelled "Other" — the bar-and-column.html §02 move. Every segment's identity is still required, or a middle segment must be compared across columns (next row).
Identity required, or a middle segment must be compared across columns Change the form: grouped bars, small multiples, or a sortable table. Nothing — this is the floor. The tooltip was never the fix for a chart carrying the wrong question.
05 / The reasoning

What the stack shows, and what it withholds

A stacked bar hides exactly what a tooltip is built to recover. The four rules below explain why the rulecard's tiers aren't arbitrary — they follow from what a stack can and cannot show on its own.

1

The floating middle segment

Only the bottom segment and the total share the zero baseline. Every segment above it starts wherever the one below ends, so the eye can judge its length within a column but not compare it across columns — a bar reads by length, so it must start at zero; a middle segment never gets one.

2

Why the tooltip earns its place

The exact per-segment number is precisely what the stack withholds. A tooltip here recovers the one fact the encoding can't show — enrichment, not a patch over a chart that shouldn't have shipped.

3

Progressive disclosure is the whole law

Always visible, on demand, on navigate — the shape outlives stacked bars. Any view that can't fit every fact on the canvas earns the same three tiers this record sets for the tooltip.

4

Interaction is not a tile-scale affordance

A hover or focus target assumes room to point at. Below the size where a segment can be labelled, a chart stops informing and starts decorating — promote it to a number before reaching for a tooltip to rescue it.

06 / What the field says

Nonessential, dismissible, never interactive

Three sources converge on the same shape. A tooltip carries nonessential enrichment, must be dismissible without losing the pointer, and never carries interactive content — VC's three-tier rule sits inside all three constraints already.

WCAG 2.1 · SC 1.4.13

Dismissible, hoverable, persistent

Content that appears on hover or focus must be dismissible without moving the pointer, stay hoverable so the pointer can reach it without it disappearing, and persist until the trigger is removed, the user dismisses it, or the content is no longer valid. It is the normative floor any hover/focus disclosure sits on.

dismissible · hoverable · persistent
W3C WAI-ARIA · APG Tooltip pattern

Not for focusable or interactive content

The pattern states plainly that "tooltip widgets do not receive focus" and that a hover containing focusable elements needs a non-modal dialog instead. A tooltip triggers on hover or keyboard focus and dismisses on Escape or on mouse-out — it is never itself a place to click.

no interactive content, ever
IBM Carbon · Tooltip usage

Contextual, helpful, nonessential

Carbon defines a tooltip as content that is "contextual, helpful, and nonessential," and rules directly against the record-dump failure mode: "do not include pertinent information for the user to complete their task," and never an interactive element — both belong in Carbon's separate toggletip component instead.

nonessential text only
Synthesis

The pattern for tools like ours

For VC's stacked-bar tooltips: one header, one total, per-segment values — enrichment the field agrees a tooltip is built to carry, never the record's only copy of a needed fact. Everything a decision turns on stays on the canvas; everything else waits for the tooltip or the table. Our usage should follow it.

the VC tooltip default
