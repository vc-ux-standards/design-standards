Numbers, units,
and empty states

Set the number to be scanned, state its unit once, and give the four data-absent states four different faces. A blank cell that reads as a zero is not a small slip — it is a wrong answer the reader trusts, and in VC a trusted wrong answer moves a decision.

Our context

VC dashboards mix rates, counts and dollars in one grid, and a quality view always has measures not yet reporting or held back for a small denominator. The deciding pressure is the reader must never mistake "no data" for a zero, or trust a rate built on a handful of members.

The rule

Set numbers in tabular figures with thousands separators, and hold decimal precision constant within a view. State the unit once — in the header or axis, not on every value — and let a rate carry its denominator (N=). Give no data, a real zero, a suppressed cell and a loading cell four distinct treatments; never a bare blank.

03 / The reasoning

Why the number and its absence both need rules

A tile is scanned in a second, and the reader acts on what it says — including when it says nothing. Every rule below protects that second: make the value legible, spend no ink twice, and never let absence pose as a value.

1

A number is read, not just shown

The eye compares digits down a column, so they must line up. Tabular figures, thousands separators, and one decimal precision held across the view — anything less and the comparison the tile exists for stops working.

2

The unit belongs once

Put %, $ PMPM or /1,000 in the header or axis. Repeating it on every value is ink competing with the number it labels; state it once and let the figure carry the tile.

3

A rate is nothing without its denominator

100% on two members is noise dressed as a result. Show N, and suppress a rate below the threshold rather than printing it — the honesty record covers why a small-N rate misleads.

4

Absence has meanings

No data, a real zero, a suppressed cell and a loading cell are four different facts. Give them four faces; a single blank collapses them and the reader guesses — usually "zero".

04 / What the field says

Formatting and empty states are settled craft

The systems agree on both halves: format numbers by type and align them, and treat an empty cell as a designed state that explains itself. Small-cell suppression is the analytics convention layered on top.

IBM · Carbon — closest to our world

Distinct empty states, numbers that scan

Carbon specifies separate empty states — no data, not configured, error — rather than one blank, and formats metrics with grouping separators and consistent precision. It is the enterprise pattern for a data grid like VC's.

distinct empties · formatted metrics
Google · Material

Format by type; the empty state is designed

Material formats numbers by their type and treats the empty state as a first-class, designed state with guidance — not an absence the layout forgot to fill.

number by type; designed empties
Nielsen Norman Group

An empty state is content

NN/g's guidance: an empty state should say why it is empty and what to do next. A cell that leaves the reader guessing whether it is zero or missing has failed that test.

explain the blank
Stephen Few · Information Dashboard Design

Legible numbers on a dashboard

Few's rules for tabular numbers — right-align, align decimals, minimise non-data ink — are exactly what lets a column of tiles compare at a glance.

align · minimise ink
Practice · small-cell suppression

Small denominators are withheld

Across analytics and health-data privacy practice, cells below a threshold (often around 11) are suppressed rather than shown — because both the rate and the privacy are unreliable at that size.

suppress below threshold

The pattern for tools like ours

For a VC quality grid: tabular numbers with the unit stated once, a rate that carries its denominator, and four honest faces for absence. Our usage should follow it.
