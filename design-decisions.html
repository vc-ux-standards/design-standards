Adopted Component usage · Steppers

When to use a vertical
versus a horizontal stepper

We ship both orientations. This sets out which to reach for, and why, anchored to what Value Connect actually is: an enterprise configuration tool. The short version is that most of our stepper usage should be vertical, and the reason is specific to our workflows, not a general style preference.

Our context

Value Connect sits in population health and value-based care, the same enterprise market as Arcadia and Innovaccer. The users are analysts, care managers and configuration admins working on large desktop monitors. The work is long, server-backed configuration: cohort and registry builders, quality-measure definition, risk-model and attribution setup, contract arrangement. Step content lives in a working canvas, never inline, and mobile use is negligible. Since the canvas holds the content for both orientations, the inline-versus-canvas question decides nothing for us. What decides it is scale, per-step status, and the freedom to retune out of order.

The rule

Default to a vertical stepper in a left rail, with step content in the canvas beside it. It fits our long, domain-heavy configuration flows: it scales past five steps, shows a name, a one-line description and a status for every step at once, and lets users jump back to retune a section. Reserve a horizontal stepper for a short, strictly linear wizard of about three to five steps with terse labels, where each step is its own page and a compact overview at the top of the canvas is all that is needed.

03 / The reasoning

Four forces, all pointing the same way for us

The decision is structural, not aesthetic. Four pressures act on our flows in particular, and for configuration work they all favour the vertical rail.

1

Scale against domain labels

Our step names are domain terms: risk adjustment methodology, attribution window, numerator logic. A horizontal bar divides its width by the step count and truncates phrases like these by the third step, and our flows routinely run past the five-step ceiling a bar can hold. Vertical gives each step the full measure and scrolls when it needs to.

2

Name, description and status, at once

Configuration steps are rarely self-explanatory. Each wants a one-line description, and each carries a state the admin must see: complete, in progress, not started, or complete with errors. Only the vertical rail shows all of that for every step simultaneously. A horizontal bar shows a number and a word, and hides which sections are still invalid.

3

A persistent map, not a thin strip

In a left rail the vertical stepper is a map of the whole process that stays beside the canvas during a long session. This is the enterprise convention, and the system built for data-heavy products, IBM Carbon, places the progress indicator in side panels and tearsheets for exactly this. A horizontal bar is a thinner top-of-page overview that suits a short page-swapping wizard, not a configuration session.

4

Non-linear retuning

Configuration is iterative: define a cohort, preview the population, go back and adjust the criteria, check again. An interactive vertical rail makes every step a clickable target with its current status, so jumping back is one click. A horizontal bar makes back-and-forth cramped and gives no per-step status to navigate by.

04 / Edge cases and anti-patterns

Where to draw the line in review

Reach for horizontal only when

  • The flow is short (three to five steps), strictly linear, and each step is its own page.
  • Labels are one to two words, with no description or per-step status to convey.
  • It is a focused, self-contained task, usually in a modal: a quick create, publish or confirm.
  • A compact progress overview at the top of the canvas is all the wayfinding the task needs.

Default to vertical when (most of our surfaces)

  • The flow has six or more steps, or groups into named phases.
  • Step names are domain phrases that need a description and a visible status.
  • Admins will revisit and retune steps out of order across a session.
  • The stepper should persist as a navigation map in a left rail beside the canvas.

Reject in review

  • Horizontal scrolling, or a horizontal bar that wraps to two lines, to fit the steps. Switch to a vertical rail and, past six or seven steps, group into phases.
  • Hiding step status. In a configuration tool, which sections still have errors is operational information the admin needs at a glance; surface complete, error and not-started states in the rail.
  • A stepper used for non-sequential navigation. Peer sections with no required order are tabs, not a stepper.
  • Contorting a horizontal bar for narrow widths. With negligible mobile use, do not compromise the desktop pattern; our only constrained contexts are side panels and split inspectors, which the vertical rail already fits.
05 / What the major design systems say

The enterprise convention, not a house opinion

The systems that enterprise data products are built on converge on the same logic: vertical scales with steps, labels and status and reads more easily; horizontal is for short wizards where one step depends on the last. Carbon, IBM's system for data-heavy products, is the closest match to our world and the clearest on the rail pattern. Links are in the references.

IBM · Carbon — closest to our world

Vertical for readability, placed in a side panel

Carbon's guidance is to arrange the progress indicator vertically when possible because it reads more easily, and it explicitly places the component on full pages, in modals, in side panels and in tearsheets. It defines complete, current, not-started and error states, and an interactive mode that lets users navigate to any step, the exact behaviours a configuration rail needs.

vertical, side panel, status, interactive
Google · Material / Angular Material

Horizontal eats width and risks a second scroll axis

Material reserves horizontal for flows where a later step depends on an earlier one. Angular's implementation note is the operative reason to avoid it at scale: horizontal steppers take significantly more horizontal space and can introduce a second scrolling dimension, which makes content harder to consume.

horizontal only for dependent steps
Atlassian Design System

Short labels, and cap the steps

Progress tracker labels stay to one or two words on a single line. Beyond six steps, Atlassian advises simplifying or breaking the task into multiple flows rather than stretching the component, which is the basis for grouping our longer flows into phases.

short labels, group past 6
Ant Design — enterprise React

Vertical is the variant that carries descriptions

Ant ships horizontal, vertical and navigation variants. The vertical direction is the one that holds a per-step description, which is the layout our domain steps require.

vertical for descriptions
MUI · Material UI

Horizontal for dependent wizards

Horizontal is well suited when the contents of one step depend on an earlier step, the short page-swapping wizard where the bar tracks position and the work happens in the region below. That is our horizontal case, and only that.

horizontal for short wizards
Synthesis

The pattern for tools like ours

For an enterprise, desktop, analyst-facing configuration tool with long flows and step-level status, the field points one way: a vertical rail by default, horizontal kept to short linear wizards. Our usage should follow it.

vertical default for Value Connect
06 / Recommendation for Value Connect

Make the vertical rail the default

The house rule

  • Vertical, in a left rail beside the canvas, is the default for configuration and setup: cohort and registry builders, quality-measure definition, risk-model and attribution setup, contract arrangement, data mapping. These flows are long, domain-labelled, status-bearing and iterative, which is what the rail is built for.
  • Horizontal is reserved for the short top-level wizard: three to five terse, strictly linear steps, each its own page, usually in a modal. A quick create, publish or confirm.
  • Group any flow beyond six or seven steps into named phases in the rail rather than stretching either component.
  • Always surface per-step status, including the error state, in the rail. In a configuration tool that is operational information, not decoration.
  • Treat mobile as out of scope for steppers. Do not compromise the desktop pattern for a viewport we do not serve; the vertical rail already handles our only narrow context, the side panel.

Draft Layout · Containers

Modal, full page,
or side panel

A first pass, pending the precedent and sourcing work the stepper record already has. The rule and the table below are provisional, meant to be argued with, not cited yet.

Our context

The same as everywhere in Value Connect: desktop, analyst and admin users, long server-backed configuration with content that lives in a canvas. That sends most heavy work to full pages, leaves modals for short interruptions, and makes the side panel the tool for work that has to sit beside the main view.

The rule (provisional)

Use a modal only for a short, self-contained task that must interrupt: a confirmation, a quick create, a decision the user has to resolve before continuing. Use a full page for anything long, multi-section, or that the user navigates within and returns to. Use a side panel when the task supports the main view and that context needs to stay visible. The deciding question is whether the task needs brief and total attention, is a workspace in its own right, or runs alongside the main work.

02 / Reasoning (draft)

Why the task decides, not the visual weight

The common error is reaching for a modal because it feels lightweight, then loading it with a multi-step form that needs scrolling, validation, and the context the modal has hidden behind it. A modal removes the page behind it, so anything that needs that context, or runs long enough that the user loses their place, belongs on a full page or in a side panel. The reverse error is sending a one-line confirmation to a full page and throwing away the user's place in their work. Match the container to the shape of the task: brief and blocking, a workspace, or a companion to the main view.
